The terms "bug" and "defect" are often used interchangeably in software development, but they can have slightly different connotations depending on the context:  
  
1. **Bug**:  
- **Definition**: A bug typically refers to an error, flaw, or fault in a software program that causes it to produce an incorrect or unexpected result or to behave in unintended ways.  
- **Common Usage**: The term is often used informally to describe any issue in the software, especially during the testing phase or after the software has been deployed.  
- **Examples**: A button that doesn't respond when clicked, a feature that crashes the application, or a calculation that gives the wrong result.  
  
2. **Defect**:  
- **Definition**: A defect generally refers to any deviation from the software requirements or specifications. It encompasses any issue where the software does not meet the intended functionality, quality, or performance standards.  
- **Common Usage**: The term is more formal and is often used in the context of quality assurance and requirements validation. A defect can be identified at any stage of the software development lifecycle.  
- **Examples**: A missing feature that was specified in the requirements, a performance issue that makes the software run slower than expected, or a security vulnerability.  
  
In summary, while both terms refer to problems in the software, "bug" is more commonly used to describe coding errors discovered during testing or usage, whereas "defect" is a broader term that includes any failure to meet requirements, whether due to design issues, coding errors, or other factors.
