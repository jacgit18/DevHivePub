Your experience supporting an **upskilling initiative** (like a bootcamp for software engineers) as a Business Systems Analyst (BSA) is **atypical but not unheard of**—most BSAs focus on **process optimization, system requirements, and stakeholder coordination** rather than leading training programs. However, the level of meeting involvement (leading vs. shadowing) often depends on **seniority, company culture, and project scope**. Here’s how your experience compares to the norm:  
  
---  
  
### **1. Typical BSA Focus Areas (Processes > People Development)**  
Most BSAs work on:  
- **Process mapping** (e.g., "How does an insurance claim move from submission to approval?").  
- **System requirements** (e.g., "What data fields does the new CRM need?").  
- **Stakeholder mediation** (e.g., translating business needs for IT teams).  
  
**Upskilling engineers is usually:**  
- Owned by **Engineering Managers, Tech Leads, or L&D (HR)**.  
- Seen as a **"people ops" or technical leadership task**, not a BSA’s core duty.  
  
**Your Case:** Supporting a bootcamp is more aligned with **Project Coordination** or **L&D Support**—valuable, but not classic BSA work.  
  
---  
  
### **2. Meeting Involvement: Junior vs. Senior BSAs**  
| **Junior BSA** | **Senior BSA** |  
|------------------------------|------------------------------|  
| ✅ Shadows meetings | ✅ Leads requirements sessions |  
| ✅ Takes notes/schedules | ✅ Negotiates with stakeholders |  
| ✅ Drafts documentation | ✅ Drives process changes |  
| ❌ Rarely owns outcomes | ✅ Accountable for solutions |  
  
**Your Experience (Scheduling, Minimal Soft Skills):**  
- Common for **entry-level/junior BSAs**, especially in structured orgs.  
- Some BSAs **never lead meetings** if the role is more technical/documentation-heavy.  
  
**But:** Even junior BSAs are **usually expected to**:  
- Ask clarifying questions in meetings.  
- Facilitate small-group discussions (e.g., "Can you walk me through this workflow?").  
  
---  
  
### **3. Why Your Role Might Have Focused on Upskilling**  
Possible reasons:  
- **Company size:** Startups/small teams often blend roles (e.g., BSA + Project Manager).  
- **Project-specific need:** If the bootcamp was tied to a **system rollout** (e.g., upskilling engineers on a new tool), the BSA might support logistics.  
- **Title misuse:** Some companies mislabel roles (e.g., calling a "Training Coordinator" a BSA).  
  
---  
  
### **4. Soft Skills in BSA Work: The Norm**  
Most BSAs **heavily rely** on soft skills like:  
- **Active listening** (to extract hidden requirements).  
- **Conflict resolution** (e.g., "The sales team wants X, but IT says it’s impossible").  
- **Stakeholder management** (managing expectations).  
  
If your role didn’t require much of this, it might have been:  
- A **very technical BSA role** (e.g., focused on data modeling).  
- A **temporary/niche assignment** (like the bootcamp project).  
  
---  
  
### **Key Takeaways**  
1. **Upskilling engineers is not a standard BSA responsibility**—but supporting it as part of a larger project (e.g., a system migration) can happen.  
2. **Junior BSAs often shadow** but should gradually take ownership of meetings. If yours didn’t, it could reflect:  
- A **highly structured hierarchy** (e.g., strict boss-led culture).  
- A **role more focused on back-end tasks** (documentation, tools).  
3. **Soft skills are usually critical**—if your role didn’t need them, it might have been an outlier.  
  
**Was your experience common?** Not quite—but it’s a **useful hybrid skillset** (BSA + project coordination). If you enjoyed the upskilling side, roles like **Technical Trainer, L&D Specialist, or Agile Coach** might interest you!
