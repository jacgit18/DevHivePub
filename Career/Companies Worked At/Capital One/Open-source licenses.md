Many companies, including prominent ones like Capital One, have reservations about using certain types of open-source licenses due to the legal and operational implications they bring. Specifically, licenses such as the Affero General Public License (AGPL) and other network copyleft licenses often pose significant concerns.

### Network Copyleft Licenses
- **AGPL**: The AGPL is designed to close the "service as a software substitute" loophole by ensuring that if a company uses AGPL-licensed software to provide a service over a network, they must also release the source code of any modifications they make. This requirement can be seen as too restrictive for companies that build proprietary solutions or offer software as a service (SaaS). They may be reluctant to use AGPL-licensed software because it obligates them to disclose their proprietary code if they modify the AGPL software.

### Non-commercial Licenses
- **Examples**: Creative Commons NonCommercial (CC BY-NC) and other similar licenses restrict the use of the software for commercial purposes. Companies typically avoid these licenses because they want the flexibility to use all software assets within their commercial operations without legal restrictions or the need for separate licensing arrangements.

### Why Companies Avoid These Licenses
1. **Legal Risks**: The obligations imposed by these licenses can create legal risks. Companies want to avoid the possibility of inadvertently violating license terms and facing legal action.
2. **Operational Constraints**: Adopting software with such licenses can impose operational constraints, especially when integrating with other proprietary systems or when scaling solutions.
3. **Cost of Compliance**: Ensuring compliance with these licenses can be costly and time-consuming. This includes monitoring usage, understanding the complex terms, and potentially having to contribute proprietary code back to the community.
4. **Competitive Advantage**: Many companies rely on proprietary software to maintain a competitive edge. Licenses like AGPL can undermine this by requiring them to release enhancements and modifications that they would prefer to keep confidential.

### Broader Industry Practice
This cautious approach is not unique to Capital One. Many companies, especially those in the finance, healthcare, and technology sectors, prefer permissive licenses (like MIT or Apache 2.0) that do not impose such stringent requirements. These licenses are seen as more business-friendly because they allow companies to use, modify, and distribute the software with fewer restrictions.

### Conclusion
In summary, the reluctance to use network copyleft and non-commercial licenses is a common practice among many companies due to the potential legal, operational, and competitive implications. By avoiding these licenses, companies like Capital One can mitigate risks, reduce compliance costs, and maintain greater control over their proprietary software and intellectual property.
