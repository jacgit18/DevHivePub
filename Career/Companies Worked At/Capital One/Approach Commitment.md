**Concept: "Follow the Leader" Experimental Testing in Engineering Workflows**

Instead of requiring _everyone_ to adopt a new experimental testing process simultaneously, consider having **one person or one team commit to the experiment first**. This creates a **proof of concept** that allows others to observe outcomes, gather insights, and build confidence before widespread adoption. This approach is especially useful in fast-evolving environments where requirements or conditions may change over time.

This model works similarly to a **"lead implementer"** or **"pilot model"**:

- One engineer or team builds and tests a new approach or framework.
    
- The goal is to **finalize a baseline concept** that others can replicate or adapt.
    
- Once validated, the process is documented and **modularized** for other teams or workflows to implement under conditional approval or in parallel development lanes.
    

This also makes room for **progressive rollouts** and **multiple downstream implementations**:

- Each team may implement variations or extensions of the initial model based on their own context.
    
- Learnings from the lead implementation inform decision-making for other processes, increasing overall system maturity.
    

**Benefits:**

- Reduces risk and resistance to change.
    
- Encourages innovation without forcing disruption.
    
- Establishes a clear reference implementation others can follow.
    
- Creates a feedback loop between the experimental and stable tracks.
    

**Food for thought while engineering:**

- What if every major change had a "designated experimenter"?
    
- How can conditional approvals or "soft opt-ins" create a safer space for innovation?
    
- What structures help signal to others when a proof-of-concept is stable enough to follow?
