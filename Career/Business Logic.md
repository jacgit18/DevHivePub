When you're testing, adding a new feature, or modifying the functionality of a codebase, it's essential to ensure that your changes align with the intended business logic. Asking questions about how something works in terms of business logic can help clarify requirements and avoid potential errors. Here's an expanded approach to how you might do this:  
  
### 1. **Understand the Business Logic First:**  
- Before diving into the technical details, make sure you have a clear understanding of the business logic related to the feature or functionality you're working on. Business logic refers to the rules and processes that dictate how data is created, stored, and modified within the application to meet business objectives.  
- Familiarize yourself with any existing documentation, user stories, or requirement specifications that outline the business logic. This will give you a foundation to ask more informed questions.  
  
### 2. **Frame Your Questions Contextually:**  
- When you're unclear about how a particular aspect of the code or feature should work, ask questions that directly relate to the business logic. For example:  
- "Is this how the data should be processed according to our business rules?"  
- "Does this condition align with the expected behavior in the user journey?"  
- "Are there any edge cases in the business logic that I should be aware of when implementing this feature?"  
- Make sure your questions are specific and relate to the scenario you're dealing with, rather than being too broad or vague.  
  
### 3. **Engage with Stakeholders:**  
- If possible, discuss the business logic with stakeholders like product managers, business analysts, or clients who are closely involved with the business requirements. This ensures that your understanding aligns with their expectations.  
- Example questions might include:  
- "Can you confirm if this workflow accurately reflects how the business operates?"  
- "If we change this part of the functionality, will it impact any other areas of the business process?"  
  
### 4. **Validate with Use Cases:**  
- Present specific use cases or scenarios to validate your understanding of the business logic. For example:  
- "In this use case, if the customer selects option A, the system should do X, correct?"  
- "For this type of transaction, the discount should only apply under these conditions, right?"  
- Use real-world examples to make the discussion more tangible and easier to understand.  
  
### 5. **Collaborate with Your Team:**  
- In a team setting, it's helpful to have collaborative discussions around business logic during code reviews or planning sessions. Encourage team members to ask similar questions and validate assumptions.  
- Example: "Is this how we agreed the business logic would handle these types of inputs?"  
- This collaborative approach helps ensure that everyone on the team has a consistent understanding of the business logic.  
  
### 6. **Document Your Findings:**  
- After clarifying the business logic, document your findings and the answers you receive. This can be useful for future reference and can help ensure consistency in the application of business rules.  
- Example: "Based on our discussion, I've noted that the system should reject any input that doesn't meet these specific criteria, as it violates the business logic."  
  
### 7. **Continuous Validation During Testing:**  
- While testing, continuously validate that the implemented code aligns with the business logic. If something seems off, revisit your understanding and ask questions again.  
- Example: "During testing, I noticed that the system allows this action, but based on the business logic, it shouldn't. Can we review this?"  
  
### 8. **Stay Open to Feedback:**  
- Be open to feedback or corrections from those more familiar with the business logic. Sometimes, what seems logical from a technical standpoint may not align with business needs, and adjustments may be necessary.  
- Example: "I thought this approach would work, but after your input, I see that it doesn't fully capture the business logic. How should we adjust it?"  
  
By consistently asking questions about how something works in terms of business logic, you can ensure that the technical implementation aligns with the desired business outcomes, leading to a more reliable and user-aligned product.
