In cases where **everything is purely factual and objective** (e.g., math, hard science, verified historical events), the statement *"Either we're both wrong or both right"* takes on a different meaning—because objective truth, by definition, doesn’t depend on perspective. Here’s how to analyze it:  

---

### **1. Both Are Right (Possible in Objective Contexts?)**  
**Only if:**  
- They are **saying the same thing in different words**.  
  - *Example:*  
    - Person A: "The Earth orbits the Sun."  
    - Person B: "The Sun is the gravitational center of our solar system."  
    - **Both are factually correct**, just phrased differently.  

- They **agree on the core fact but disagree on trivial details**.  
  - *Example:*  
    - Person A: "Water boils at 100°C at sea level."  
    - Person B: "Water’s boiling point is 212°F at standard pressure."  
    - **Both are right**—they’re just using different units.  

**But:** If they’re making **contradictory factual claims**, they **cannot both be right** in an objective framework.  

---

### **2. Both Are Wrong (More Likely in Objective Contexts)**  
**This happens when:**  
- **Both sides misunderstand the facts.**  
  - *Example:*  
    - Person A: "Light is only a wave."  
    - Person B: "Light is only a particle."  
    - **Both are wrong**—light behaves as both (wave-particle duality).  

- **Both rely on outdated/debunked information.**  
  - *Example (historical):*  
    - Person A: "Phrenology accurately measures intelligence."  
    - Person B: "Intelligence is determined solely by skull shape."  
    - **Both are wrong**—phrenology is pseudoscience.  

- **They’re arguing about a false premise.**  
  - *Example:*  
    - Person A: "This medicine cures all diseases."  
    - Person B: "No, it cures 99% of diseases."  
    - If the medicine is a **placebo**, **both claims are wrong**.  

---

### **Key Implications for Factual Debates**  
1. **Objective truth is binary in hard facts**—if two claims contradict, at least one must be wrong.  
2. **Both can be wrong if neither aligns with reality** (common in myths, oversimplifications, or outdated beliefs).  
3. **Nuance matters**—sometimes disagreements stem from miscommunication, not factual errors.  

---

### **When This Phrase Still Applies (Even in Objective Contexts)**  
- **If the ‘facts’ are uncertain or disputed** (e.g., cutting-edge science, conflicting studies).  
- **If the debate is about interpretation of facts** (e.g., "Does this data prove X or Y?").  
- **If both sides are using flawed logic** (e.g., straw-manning each other’s arguments).  

---

### **Final Thought**  
In purely factual domains, the phrase *"Both could be wrong"* is often more useful than *"Both could be right."* It encourages **humility and verification**—because in science, history, and math, reality doesn’t compromise.  

**Example in Action:**  
- *Person A:* "The universe is 6,000 years old."  
- *Person B:* "No, it’s exactly 13.8 billion years old."  
- **If new evidence emerges showing the universe is 13.6 billion years old**, **both were slightly wrong**.
