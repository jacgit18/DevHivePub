When telling a story, don't assume your audience is already interested—you need to create intrigue and build curiosity. Start by introducing a gap in the story, something that leaves them wondering, and focus on conveying the emotions you experienced in the moment to draw them in. Also start with the end in mind.

Keep the story concise. Drop small hints about the outcome without giving away the punchline or the lesson right away, so they stay engaged until the end.

**Example:**  
_"Yeah, I've heard about them. They're probably not a big fan of me—I might've pissed them off pretty badly."_

This approach creates curiosity, leaving space for the listener to ask follow-up questions like _"Wait, what happened?"_ or _"Why do you say that?"_ Let their interest guide the conversation, rather than overexplaining upfront.



### Delayed Revelation

1. **Set the Hook Early**: Start with an engaging or intriguing premise but leave out a key detail. This draws your audience in because they’ll want to fill in the gaps.
    
    - Example: “I once got into trouble at work for something _completely ridiculous_... but I’ll get to that.”
2. **Plant Curiosity Seeds**: Use vague or intentionally misleading language to keep them guessing without overcomplicating the setup.
    
    - Example: “You won’t believe who I ran into yesterday… but let me explain the setup first.”
3. **Build a Layer of Misdirection**: Let the audience make assumptions about where the story or joke is going by omitting key context until the reveal.
    
    - Example: “So I’m at this wedding, and this guy starts shouting at me—turns out, _he thought I was someone else_.”
4. **Time the Reveal**: Save the critical detail for when it creates the biggest impact, whether for a laugh, gasp, or "aha" moment.
    
    - Example: “...and that’s when I realized I was at the _wrong wedding_.”
5. **Use the Rule of Three**: If telling a longer story, set up two small but related details or events before delivering the punchline or revelation on the third.
    
    - Example: “First, I forgot my wallet. Then, I spilled coffee on my shirt. But the real kicker? I walked into my meeting... wearing two different shoes.”
6. **Pair It with Humor or Drama**: When delaying a detail, emphasize the emotional reaction it’ll provoke. This strengthens the delivery.
    
    - Example (humor): “I told my boss I’d fix it immediately. What I didn’t tell him? _I had no idea how to fix it_.”
    - Example (drama): “She didn’t say a word... until she finally whispered, _‘I know what you did.’_”
